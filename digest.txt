Biology's Waymo Moment: Ginkgo Bioworks' Jason Kelly

In this episode of *Training Data*, Ginkgo Bioworks CEO Jason Kelly argues that AI represents a fundamentally different inflection point for biology and biopharma — unlike the internet or social media, which he dismisses as "back office IT crap" for the industry. Kelly recounts Ginkgo's unlikely origin story: bootstrapping for six years out of grad school before a 2014 Sam Altman blog post led to a YC stint that finally unlocked capital. He explains that Ginkgo's core mission is making biology easier to engineer by treating DNA as programmable code, pursuing two parallel tracks — better biological design (now largely ceded to players like Chai Bio and Arc Institute's EVO 2) and cheaper, faster physical lab testing. Notably, Kelly reveals he has deliberately narrowed Ginkgo's focus to the "back-end" lab automation and AI-assisted testing problem, framing it as an engineering challenge that can be "rendered to dust," and citing a recent collaboration with OpenAI as evidence of this strategic pivot.

在这期《Training Data》播客中，Ginkgo Bioworks首席执行官Jason Kelly提出，AI对生物学和生物制药行业而言是一个根本性的转折点——与互联网或社交媒体不同，他将后者对该行业的影响斥为"后台IT杂务"。Kelly回顾了Ginkgo的创业历程：从研究生院直接创业，艰难自力更生六年，直到2014年Sam Altman的一篇博文促成其加入YC，才真正打开融资局面。他将DNA类比为可编程代码，阐述了Ginkgo的双核战略——优化生物设计（现已基本让位于Chai Bio、Arc Institute的EVO 2等外部团队）以及降低实验室物理测试的成本与周期。值得关注的是，Kelly透露他已将Ginkgo的重心战略性地收窄至"后端"实验室自动化与AI辅助测试领域，将其定性为可被彻底攻克的工程问题，并以近期与OpenAI的合作项目为佐证。
