**Training Data — Biology's Waymo Moment: Ginkgo Bioworks' Jason Kelly**
https://youtube.com/watch?v=g45Alfg7diw

**The Takeaway:** AI reasoning models running robotic labs can already beat human scientists at experimental design — and the implications for how we fund and run all of science are enormous.

Jason Kelly is the founder and CEO of Ginkgo Bioworks, a company he started straight out of grad school in 2008 with the audacious goal of making biology as programmable as software. After six years of bootstrapping on government grants and a chance encounter with a young Sam Altman at YC, Ginkgo has spent 15 years building both sides of the biology-engineering stack: smarter design tools and cheaper, faster lab automation.

The story Kelly tells here is about a recent collaboration with OpenAI that quietly upended his priors. His team handed an AI reasoning model control of a robotic lab and set it loose on a benchmark protein synthesis problem — optimizing "cell-free synthesis," essentially programming cell guts in a test tube to cheaply produce proteins. The model ran 384-well-plate experiments in iterative rounds, got the data back, designed the next set. After four rounds, it beat the Stanford state-of-the-art. After six rounds, it beat it by **40%**.

"It can run a lab 24 hours a day. What really let it break through wasn't that it was so smart — it was that it could run experiments. And the question was just could it design them like a scientist could? And the answer was yeah, hell yeah it could."

But the bigger point Kelly makes is structural: modern science is shockingly inefficient. Less than 5% of research budgets go to actual reagents — the chemicals you literally consume doing experiments. The rest is overhead: people, lab space, equipment that sits idle 5 days a week, duplicated across every lab that does similar work. An AI-run robotic lab flips that ratio. You'd budget by reagent cost — usage-based pricing for science. That's a potential 10x increase in data per dollar, even if the AI is no smarter than the humans it replaces.

The vision he sketches: 100 AI scientists pursuing 100 parallel Alzheimer's hypotheses on the same robotic infrastructure, sharing raw experimental data daily (not just published papers every 18 months), cross-pollinating each other's failed results. No incremental tweak — a fundamentally different epistemology for experimental science.

Kelly is emphatic that this is different from every previous tech wave: "All of the previous revolutions in tech — the internet, social media, whatever — have been totally meaningless to biotechnology and biopharma. Not this. This is actually going to change the fundamentals of how we do science."

*中文版 / Chinese*

**要点：** AI 推理模型驱动机器人实验室，已经能在实验设计上超越人类科学家——而这对整个科学体系的资助和运作方式，意味着一场根本性的颠覆。

Jason Kelly 是 Ginkgo Bioworks 的创始人兼 CEO。他 2008 年博士毕业后直接创办了这家公司，目标是让生物学像软件一样可编程。熬过六年政府拨款支撑的艰难岁月，偶遇年轻的 Sam Altman 并入选 YC 后，Ginkgo 用了 15 年时间打磨生物工程的两个核心环节：更聪明的设计工具，以及更快更便宜的实验室自动化。

Kelly 讲述的这个故事，来自最近与 OpenAI 的合作，结果出乎他自己意料。他的团队让一个 AI 推理模型接管了机器人实验室，去攻克一个蛋白质合成的基准测试——"无细胞合成"，就是用细胞内部的分子机器在试管里廉价生产蛋白质。模型以 384 孔板为单位迭代实验，拿到数据，设计下一轮方案。四轮之后，超越了斯坦福的最优记录。六轮之后，**超越了 40%**。

"它能 24 小时运转实验室。真正让它突破的不是有多聪明，而是它能做实验。问题只是它能不能像科学家一样设计实验？答案是：可以，完全可以。"

但 Kelly 更大的论点是关于结构性低效：现代科学在烧钱方式上极度低效。不到 5% 的研究预算用在了真正做实验消耗的试剂上，其余全是开销：人员、实验室场地、各家重复购置的闲置设备。AI 驱动的机器人实验室会颠覆这个比例——你将按试剂成本来预算，这是科学的按量计费模式，每一分钱的数据产出可能提升 10 倍，哪怕 AI 不比人更聪明。

他描绘的愿景：100 个 AI 科学家在同一套机器人基础设施上并行推进 100 个阿兹海默症假说，每天共享原始实验数据（而不是等 18 个月后才发一篇论文），彼此的失败结果也能互相启发。这不是渐进式改良，而是实验科学认知方式的根本性变革。

Kelly 的判断非常明确：之前所有的技术革命，互联网、社交媒体，对生物技术和制药行业都无足轻重，不过是些"后台 IT 杂活"。这一次不同——AI 真的会改变科学本身的运作方式。
